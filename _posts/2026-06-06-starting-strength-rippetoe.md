**Starting Strength: Basic Barbell Training**  
By Mark Rippetoe (3rd ed., with contributions from Lon Kilgore and others) · The standard introduction to barbell training for novices · Core idea: **a few compound lifts, trained with correct technique, progressed linearly while recovery still permits**

## Overview

*Starting Strength* is not a magazine workout or a collection of muscle-isolation routines. It is a **minimal program built on biomechanics**: squat, press, bench press, deadlift, and power clean—performed for sets across a full range of motion, with weight added session by session while the trainee is still a **novice** (someone who recovers from stress quickly enough to gain strength every workout).

Rippetoe’s argument is structural:

> Strength is the productive adaptation of the body to stress. The program must deliver stress, allow recovery, and repeat—before the adaptation window closes.

The book is half **instruction manual** (how to squat, press, pull, and clean) and half **theory of training** (stress, recovery, adaptation; novice vs intermediate vs advanced). That makes it unusually durable: even readers who later switch programs often keep the lift models and the habit of **adding weight only when form holds**.

Three questions to carry while reading: **Is the bar path correct? Am I recovering between sessions? When linear progression stops, what changes—not just what weight goes on the bar?**

## Who this book is for

| | |
|---|---|
| **Good fit** | Adults new to barbells; lifters tired of random programs; anyone who wants a clear default before specializing |
| **When to use** | After medical clearance for resistance training; when you can train 3×/week consistently |
| **What you get** | Detailed lift models, novice program, vocabulary for stress/recovery/adaptation |
| **What it is not** | Physique contest prep, Olympic weightlifting specialization, rehab protocol, or machine-circuit fitness |

**Read if you:**
- Want one book that teaches **how to lift** and **how to program** at the same time
- Prefer compound barbell movements over exercise variety for its own sake
- Need a shared language with coaches or training partners (“novice LP,” “reset,” “microload”)

**Wait or supplement if you:**
- Cannot squat or deadlift without pain—get assessed first; technique chapters help but are not medicine
- Need sport-specific speed, agility, or endurance—add those **after** a strength base or in parallel with managed volume
- Want minimalist home training without a rack—SS assumes a proper gym setup

## The five lifts (why these, not others)

Rippetoe selects lifts that use **the most muscle mass over the longest effective range of motion**, loaded with a barbell:

| Lift | Role | Program note |
|------|------|----------------|
| **Squat** | Primary lower-body strength | High frequency (every session) drives adaptation |
| **Press** | Overhead strength | Strict standing press; no leg drive |
| **Bench press** | Horizontal push | Paired with press on alternate days |
| **Deadlift** | Posterior chain pull | One heavy set; high systemic stress |
| **Power clean** | Explosive pull + rack | Teaches acceleration; optional swap to barbell row in some variants |

Machines and cables are not evil—they are **poor teachers of balance and rigid midline stabilization** for a novice learning to move load with a barbell. SS is opinionated here on purpose.

## Novice program structure

### A/B template

Typical week:

```text
Monday    — Workout A: Squat, Press, Deadlift
Wednesday — Workout B: Squat, Bench, Power Clean
Friday    — Workout A (weights increased from Monday)
```

Next week begins with **Workout B** on Monday. Squat appears every session because squat stress is the main driver of systemic adaptation in the novice phase.

### Sets, reps, progression

| Lift | Work sets | Typical progression |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Squat | 3×5 | +5 lb / session |
| Press | 3×5 | +5 lb / session |
| Bench | 3×5 | +5 lb / session |
| Deadlift | 1×5 | +10 lb / session |
| Power clean | 5×3 | +2.5 lb / session |

When all prescribed reps are completed with acceptable form, add weight next time. When reps fail, **reset** (drop ~10% and rebuild) or **microload** (smaller jumps) before abandoning linear progression.

### When novice LP ends

You are no longer a novice when you **cannot recover** enough to add weight each session—often after weeks or a few months, highly individual. The book points toward **intermediate programming** (e.g. weekly progression) covered more fully in *Practical Programming*—SS gives the exit ramp, not the full map.

## Technique: what “model” means

Each lift chapter follows a pattern:

1. **Biomechanical rationale** — moment arms, balance, bar path  
2. **Setup checklist** — feet, grip, breath, eye gaze  
3. **Execution cues** — hips, knees, bar over mid-foot, etc.  
4. **Common faults** — depth, back angle, early arm pull  

The “model” is not aesthetic perfection for Instagram. It is **repeatable positions that keep the load on the right tissues** and let force be applied safely as weight climbs.

**Practical rule:** film sets from the side; compare to the book’s fault diagrams. Coaching beats solo guessing—especially for squat and clean.

## Recovery, nutrition, and lifestyle

Strength is not built in the gym; it is **revealed** there and **built** during recovery.

| Factor | SS emphasis | Common failure |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| **Sleep** | Non-negotiable for novices adding load | Training through chronic sleep debt |
| **Food** | Enough protein and total calories to grow | Under-eating while expecting +5 lb jumps |
| **Stress** | Systemic load includes work and life | Treating gym as isolated from everything else |
| **Rest between sets** | Long enough to complete next set | Turning strength work into conditioning |

The internet-era “GOMAD” (gallon of milk a day) meme came from SS culture—it illustrates **eat to support the bar**, not a universal prescription. Adjust for your body, labs, and goals.

## Stress · recovery · adaptation (the theory loop)

```text
Training stress  →  Recovery  →  Adaptation (stronger)
        ↑___________________________________|
              (next session adds stress)
```

This loop is why **more exercises** are not better for a novice: they fragment stress and delay recovery. It also mirrors why [有效复盘]({{ '/2026/05/24/effective-retrospective.html' | relative_url }}) matters for lifters—without a log, you cannot tell whether failure was **technique**, **under-recovery**, or **program mismatch**.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| **Ego loading** | Form breaks; missed reps pile up | Drop weight; rebuild; film sets |
| **Program hopping** | No linear history to diagnose | Run SS long enough to stall honestly |
| **Accessory creep** | Fatigue without proportional strength gain | Trust the minimum effective dose early |
| **Ignoring pain** | Tissue injury vs normal exertion blurred | Stop aggravating patterns; seek professional assessment |
| **No exit plan** | Panic when LP stops | Read intermediate transition; plan next program |
| **Skipping press** | Bench-only culture | Press builds shoulder strength and balance |

## Reading and training path

| Stage | Focus | Why |
|-------|-------|-----|
| **1. Read Part I–II** | Lifting theory + squat/deadlift | Establish why before how |
| **2. Train 2 weeks light** | Practice setup with empty bar / light loads | Motor learning before stress |
| **3. Run full NLP** | Log every session | Data for resets and nutrition tweaks |
| **4. Film monthly** | Compare to model | Catch drift before injury |
| **5. On stall** | *Practical Programming* or coached intermediate plan | LP is a phase, not a religion |

## Critiques worth keeping

Rippetoe is influential because the **defaults work for many novices**, not because every claim ages perfectly:

- **Dogmatic tone** — useful for beginners who need one answer; less useful for edge cases (mobility limits, older trainees, specific sports).  
- **Nutrition generalizations** — principles are sound; prescriptions need individualization.  
- **Power clean difficulty** — many novices benefit from a coach; barbell row substitutions exist in the ecosystem.  
- **Gender and body-composition discourse** — read for lifting mechanics; filter social commentary critically.

Treat SS as a **strong first operating system**, then upgrade when your data says so.

## Four questions after each month

1. **Are all work sets still moving with the model?**  
2. **Is weight still increasing on schedule, or stalling for a reason I can name?**  
3. **Am I sleeping and eating to match the bar?**  
4. **If LP ended, what is the next program—and why?**
