**Built to Move: The Ten Essential Habits to Help You Move Freely and Live Fully**  
By Kelly Starrett & Juliet Starrett (2023) · From the *Ready State* / mobility culture · Core idea: **longevity is a movement problem you can score, practice daily, and retest—without living in a gym**

## Overview

After decades of coaching athletes and desk workers, the Starretts noticed a pattern: people either **ignored mobility** until something hurt, or treated it as a **90-minute yoga penance** they could not sustain. *Built to Move* compresses the message into **ten vital signs**—simple assessments—and **daily minimums** that stack onto real life: meetings, parenting, travel, aging.

The book is not a replacement for strength training. It is the **operating system for joints, breathing, and walking** that makes strength programs survivable for decades.

> You are not fragile; you are **under-practiced**. Move a little, often, with tests that tell the truth.

Three questions while reading: **Which vital sign is my bottleneck? What is the smallest daily habit I will actually do? Am I retesting or just consuming mobility content?**

## The ten vital signs (framework)

Exact protocols are in the book; conceptually:

| Vital sign | What it reveals |
|------------|-----------------|
| **Breathing** | Diaphragm and rib cage capacity—foundation for bracing |
| **Sit-rise test** | Floor-to-stand without hands—global mobility + strength |
| **Extension** | Hip/spine extension—counter sitting flexion |
| **Shoulder rotation** | Overhead and rotational readiness |
| **Single-leg balance** | Proprioception, ankle/hip stability |
| **Resting squat** | Ankle/hip depth for daily life |
| **Walking / steps** | Aerobic baseline and joint nutrition |
| **Grip strength** | All-cause mortality correlate; practical strength |
| **Hip flexion** | Sitting toll on hips and back |
| **Sleep** | Recovery without which mobility work fails |

**Practice loop:** baseline score → one or two daily snacks → retest quarterly.

## Daily movement diet

Starretts argue for **minimum effective movement** embedded in the day:

```text
8,000+ steps (more if life allows)
Micro-breaks every 30–45 min at desk
Floor time (sit on ground, shoes off when safe)
10-minute mobility “snack” (target weakest vital sign)
```

This is how mobility becomes **infrastructure**, not a weekend event.

### Habit stacking examples

| Anchor | Stack |
|--------|-------|
| Morning coffee | 2 min breathing drill |
| Work timer | 5 squats + shoulder CARs |
| Evening TV | Floor sit instead of couch only |
| Post-lift | 5 min extension + hip flexor opener |

## Sit-rise test (why it matters)

Popularized in longevity research, the **sit-rise test** is a blunt instrument: can you lower to the floor and stand without support? Low scores correlate with mortality risk in epidemiology—not because the test is magic, but because it bundles **strength, balance, and mobility**.

*Built to Move* uses it as a **north star**, not a shame club. Train toward it with [Starting Strength]({{ '/2026/06/06/starting-strength-rippetoe/' | relative_url }})-style strength plus daily snacks.

## Critiques worth keeping

- **Corporate wellness tone** can feel simplified for serious athletes.  
- **Tests are proxies**, not diagnoses.  
- **Kelly’s earlier catalog** (*Supple Leopard*) is denser; *Built to Move* trades depth for adherence.

Use it to **keep moving between birthdays**, not to win Instagram contortion points.
